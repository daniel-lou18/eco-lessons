# Part 4 — The balance sheet

If the business engine is about **flow**, the balance sheet is about **stock**.

This distinction is very important.

```text
Income statement / cash flow statement = what happened over a period
Balance sheet = what exists at a point in time
```

Example:

```text
Revenue in 2025 = flow over a year
Cash on 31 Dec 2025 = stock at one date
Debt on 31 Dec 2025 = stock at one date
```

The balance sheet tells you:

> What the company owns, what it owes, and what belongs to shareholders.

The teaching sequence should be:

```text
Assets → Liabilities → Equity → Debt → Cash → Enterprise value
```

---

# 1. Assets: “What does the company own or control?”

Assets are resources the company uses to operate.

```text
Assets = things the company owns or controls that have economic value
```

Examples:

```text
cash
inventory
factories
machines
software
patents
brands
customer receivables
investments
real estate
```

Teach assets in two broad categories.

## Current assets

Assets expected to become cash or be used within roughly one year.

```text
cash
accounts receivable
inventory
short-term investments
```

These matter for liquidity.

---

## Long-term assets

Assets used over several years.

```text
factories
equipment
real estate
software
intangible assets
goodwill
long-term investments
```

These matter for business model and capital intensity.

Teaching point:

> Some businesses need many assets to grow. Others need very few.

Example:

```text
Steel producer → asset-heavy
Software company → asset-light
Bank → balance-sheet-heavy
Luxury brand → intangible-asset-heavy
Retailer → inventory-heavy
```

This prepares the learner for why ratios differ by sector.

---

# 2. Liabilities: “What does the company owe?”

Liabilities are obligations.

```text
Liabilities = money or services the company owes to others
```

Examples:

```text
supplier bills
employee wages owed
taxes owed
bank loans
bonds
lease obligations
customer deposits
pension obligations
```

Again, teach two categories.

## Current liabilities

Due within roughly one year.

```text
supplier bills
short-term debt
taxes payable
wages payable
current lease payments
```

These matter for short-term survival.

---

## Long-term liabilities

Due later.

```text
long-term debt
bonds
long-term leases
pension obligations
deferred taxes
```

These matter for long-term solvency.

Teaching point:

> Liabilities are not automatically bad. The question is whether the company can comfortably meet them.

---

# 3. Equity: “What belongs to shareholders?”

Now introduce the basic accounting equation.

```text
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

Or:

```text
Equity = Assets - Liabilities
```

Equity is the residual claim.

If the company sold all assets and paid all liabilities, what remains would theoretically belong to shareholders.

This unlocks:

```text
book value
price/book
ROE
debt/equity
shareholder capital
retained earnings
```

Teaching point:

> Equity is not the same as market value.

Book equity is accounting value.

Market cap is what investors currently pay for the company’s equity in the market.

This distinction is crucial.

---

# 4. Debt: “How much borrowed money supports the machine?”

Debt is a liability, but it deserves special attention.

```text
Debt = borrowed money that must be repaid, usually with interest
```

Debt can help a company grow, but it also increases fragility.

Teach debt with three questions:

```text
1. How much debt?
2. What interest cost?
3. When must it be repaid?
```

A company with debt is not automatically risky. A company with too much debt relative to cash flow is risky.

This leads to:

```text
debt/equity
net debt
net debt/EBITDA
interest coverage
debt maturity profile
credit rating
```

The most intuitive beginner bridge:

```text
Debt is dangerous when fixed obligations become too large compared to the cash the business generates.
```

---

# 5. Cash: “What is the financial buffer?”

Cash is an asset, but it also deserves special attention.

```text
Cash = immediate financial flexibility
```

Cash can be used to:

```text
pay bills
repay debt
invest
survive downturns
pay dividends
buy back shares
make acquisitions
```

Cash reduces financial risk.

That leads to:

```text
net debt = total debt - cash
```

Example:

```text
Company A:
Debt: €100m
Cash: €80m
Net debt: €20m

Company B:
Debt: €100m
Cash: €5m
Net debt: €95m
```

Both have €100m of debt, but Company A is much less financially stretched.

This is a very important teaching example.

---

# 6. Enterprise value: “What would it cost to buy the whole business?”

This is one of the best concepts to teach after debt and cash.

Market cap only tells you the value of the equity.

But if you buy a whole company, you also inherit its debt and receive its cash.

```text
Enterprise value = market cap + debt - cash
```

Simplified version:

```text
EV = equity value + net debt
```

Example:

```text
Company A:
Market cap: €1,000m
Debt: €300m
Cash: €100m

Enterprise value = €1,000m + €300m - €100m
Enterprise value = €1,200m
```

Teaching point:

> Market cap is the price of the shares. Enterprise value is closer to the price of the whole business.

This unlocks:

```text
EV/EBITDA
EV/sales
EV/FCF
acquisition valuation
capital-structure-neutral comparison
```

This is especially useful because two companies can have the same market cap but very different debt burdens.

# The balance-sheet teaching map

```text
Assets
→ What the company owns

Liabilities
→ What the company owes

Equity
→ What remains for shareholders

Debt
→ Borrowed money with obligations

Cash
→ Financial buffer and flexibility

Enterprise value
→ Value of the whole business, adjusted for debt and cash
```

From this, the learner can derive:

```text
Book value
= assets - liabilities

Debt/equity
= debt compared to shareholder capital

Net debt
= debt - cash

Net debt/EBITDA
= debt burden compared to operating profit proxy

Interest coverage
= ability to pay interest

Current ratio
= short-term assets / short-term liabilities

Price/book
= market price compared to accounting equity

Enterprise value
= market cap + debt - cash

EV/EBITDA
= whole-company price / operating profit proxy
```

# The big connection: business engine + balance sheet

This is where the teaching becomes powerful.

The business engine tells you:

```text
Can the company generate profit and cash?
```

The balance sheet tells you:

```text
Is the company financially strong enough to survive and reinvest?
```

Together:

```text
Business engine = earning power
Balance sheet = financial structure
```

A company can have:

## Strong engine, weak balance sheet

```text
High revenue
High margins
Good profit
But too much debt
```

This company may be attractive but financially fragile.

## Weak engine, strong balance sheet

```text
Low growth
Weak profit
But lots of cash and little debt
```

This company may survive but not compound well.

## Strong engine, strong balance sheet

```text
Growing revenue
High margins
Strong cash flow
Low debt
Lots of reinvestment opportunities
```

This is the “quality compounder” profile.

## Weak engine, weak balance sheet

```text
Falling revenue
Low margins
Negative cash flow
High debt
Little cash
```

This is the danger zone.

# Teaching the key ratios from these two modules

Once the learner understands the business engine and the balance sheet, many ratios become obvious.

## Margin ratios

Parent concepts:

```text
revenue
profit
cash flow
```

Derived metrics:

```text
gross margin = gross profit / revenue
operating margin = operating profit / revenue
net margin = net profit / revenue
FCF margin = free cash flow / revenue
```

Question answered:

> How efficient is the business?

---

## Growth ratios

Parent concepts:

```text
revenue
profit
cash flow
time
```

Derived metrics:

```text
revenue growth
earnings growth
free cash flow growth
margin expansion
```

Question answered:

> Is the business engine getting bigger or better?

---

## Profitability ratios

Parent concepts:

```text
profit
equity
invested capital
assets
```

Derived metrics:

```text
ROE = profit / equity
ROA = profit / assets
ROIC = operating profit after tax / invested capital
```

Question answered:

> How well does the company use capital?

---

## Leverage ratios

Parent concepts:

```text
debt
equity
cash
profit
cash flow
```

Derived metrics:

```text
debt/equity = debt / equity
net debt = debt - cash
net debt/EBITDA = net debt / EBITDA
interest coverage = operating profit / interest expense
```

Question answered:

> Is the company financially stretched?

---

## Valuation ratios

Parent concepts:

```text
price
market cap
enterprise value
earnings
sales
cash flow
book value
```

Derived metrics:

```text
P/E = price / earnings per share
P/S = market cap / revenue
P/B = market cap / book equity
FCF yield = free cash flow / market cap
EV/EBITDA = enterprise value / EBITDA
EV/sales = enterprise value / revenue
```

Question answered:

> How much is the market asking me to pay for the business engine?

---

# The ideal teaching sequence for these two parts

I would teach it in this order:

```text
1. Revenue
2. Costs
3. Gross profit
4. Operating profit
5. Net profit
6. Cash flow
7. Free cash flow
8. Margins
9. Reinvestment
10. Assets
11. Liabilities
12. Equity
13. Debt
14. Cash
15. Net debt
16. Enterprise value
```

Why this order?

Because it moves from intuitive to abstract.

```text
Money comes in.
Money goes out.
Some is left.
But accounting profit is not the same as cash.
Cash can be reinvested.
The company owns things.
The company owes things.
The difference belongs to shareholders.
Debt and cash change financial risk.
Enterprise value estimates the whole-business price.
```

That is a natural conceptual story.

# A simple teaching case

Use one fictional company throughout.

## Step 1: Business engine

```text
Company Alpha

Revenue: €1,000
Direct costs: €400
Gross profit: €600

Operating expenses: €300
Operating profit: €300

Interest: €50
Taxes: €50
Net profit: €200

Operating cash flow: €250
Capex: €80
Free cash flow: €170
```

Now ask:

```text
Gross margin?
Operating margin?
Net margin?
FCF margin?
```

Answers:

```text
Gross margin = 600 / 1000 = 60%
Operating margin = 300 / 1000 = 30%
Net margin = 200 / 1000 = 20%
FCF margin = 170 / 1000 = 17%
```

The learner now understands margins.

---

## Step 2: Balance sheet

```text
Company Alpha

Assets: €2,000
Liabilities: €800
Equity: €1,200

Debt: €500
Cash: €200
Net debt: €300
```

Ask:

```text
What is equity?
What is net debt?
Is the company highly indebted?
```

Then add market value:

```text
Market cap: €2,500
Debt: €500
Cash: €200

Enterprise value = 2,500 + 500 - 200 = €2,800
```

Now the learner understands market cap vs enterprise value.

---

## Step 3: Connect valuation

Using the same company:

```text
Market cap: €2,500
Enterprise value: €2,800
Revenue: €1,000
Net profit: €200
Free cash flow: €170
Operating profit: €300
```

Then derive:

```text
P/E = 2,500 / 200 = 12.5
P/S = 2,500 / 1,000 = 2.5
FCF yield = 170 / 2,500 = 6.8%
EV/Operating Profit = 2,800 / 300 = 9.3
```

This is very effective because the learner sees that valuation ratios are not random. They are just market value compared with parts of the business engine.

---

# The Pareto takeaway

For these two modules, the highest bang-for-buck concepts are:

```text
Revenue
Profit
Cash flow
Margin
Reinvestment
Assets
Liabilities
Equity
Debt
Cash
Enterprise value
```

With those, the learner can deduce most of the fundamental-analysis vocabulary.

The deepest lesson is:

> A company is valuable because of the cash its business engine can generate over time, but its balance sheet determines how resilient, risky, and flexible that engine is.

Everything else is a refinement of that sentence.
