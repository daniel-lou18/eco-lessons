Yes. These two parts are probably the **core bridge** between “I understand stock prices” and “I understand company fundamentals”.

The best way to teach them is not as accounting. Teach them as a **machine**.

# Part 3 — The business engine

The central metaphor:

> A company is a machine that takes money in, spends money to operate, keeps some profit, converts some of that profit into cash, and then decides what to do with that cash.

So the teaching sequence should be:

```text
Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment
```

Each concept should feel like the natural consequence of the previous one.

---

# 1. Revenue: “How much money enters the machine?”

Start with revenue because it is the top of the funnel.

```text
Revenue = money received from selling goods or services
```

Example:

```text
A company sells 1,000 products at €100 each.

Revenue = 1,000 × €100 = €100,000
```

The first key distinction:

```text
Revenue is not profit.
```

A company with huge revenue can still lose money.

Teaching point:

> Revenue tells you the size and growth of the business, but not whether it is efficient or profitable.

Useful questions:

```text
Is revenue growing?
Is revenue recurring or one-off?
Is revenue diversified or dependent on a few customers?
Is revenue cyclical or stable?
```

Later concepts that piggyback on revenue:

```text
revenue growth
price/sales ratio
gross margin
operating margin
net margin
free cash flow margin
```

So revenue is the base denominator for many future concepts.

---

# 2. Costs: “What does the machine consume?”

Once money enters the company, some of it immediately leaves.

Teach costs in layers.

```text
Revenue
- Cost of goods sold
= Gross profit

Gross profit
- Operating expenses
= Operating profit

Operating profit
- Interest
- Taxes
= Net profit
```

This layered approach is extremely useful because it creates a map.

## Cost layer 1: direct costs

These are costs directly tied to producing or delivering the product.

Examples:

```text
raw materials
manufacturing
shipping
cloud infrastructure
payment processing
direct labor
```

If a company sells a product for €100 and the product costs €60 to make:

```text
Revenue:       €100
Direct costs: -€60
Gross profit:  €40
```

This leads naturally to gross margin.

---

## Cost layer 2: operating costs

These are costs needed to run the business.

Examples:

```text
salaries
sales and marketing
research and development
rent
software
administration
customer support
```

Then:

```text
Gross profit
- Operating costs
= Operating profit
```

This teaches the learner that two companies can have the same gross profit but very different operating efficiency.

---

## Cost layer 3: financial and tax costs

After operating profit, the company still has to pay things like:

```text
interest on debt
taxes
exceptional charges
```

Then you get:

```text
Net profit
```

The important teaching point:

> Profit is not one number. There are layers of profit.

This prepares the learner for:

```text
gross profit
operating profit
EBIT
EBITDA
net income
earnings per share
```

---

# 3. Profit: “What does the machine keep?”

Profit is what remains after costs.

But teach profit in three layers:

```text
Gross profit
Operating profit
Net profit
```

## Gross profit

```text
Gross profit = revenue - direct costs
```

This answers:

> Is the core product or service economically attractive?

A software company may have high gross margins because each extra user costs little to serve.

A supermarket may have low gross margins because products are bought and resold at a small markup.

---

## Operating profit

```text
Operating profit = gross profit - operating expenses
```

This answers:

> Is the business model profitable after running the company?

This is often more meaningful than gross profit.

A company may have high gross margins but spend too much on marketing, R&D, or administration.

---

## Net profit

```text
Net profit = profit after all expenses, interest, taxes, and other items
```

This answers:

> What is left for shareholders according to accounting rules?

This naturally leads to:

```text
EPS = net profit / number of shares
```

And then:

```text
P/E = price / earnings per share
```

So instead of teaching P/E as a formula, you derive it from the business engine.

---

# 4. Cash flow: “Did the machine actually produce cash?”

This is the most important conceptual leap.

Profit is accounting.

Cash flow is money moving.

A company can be profitable but short on cash. A company can also have negative profit while still generating cash in some periods because of accounting effects.

Teach this distinction early.

```text
Profit ≠ cash flow
```

Simple example:

```text
A company sells €100,000 of goods in December.
The customer will pay in February.

Accounting profit may appear now.
Cash arrives later.
```

Another example:

```text
A company buys a machine for €1,000,000.
Accounting spreads the cost over several years.
But the cash may leave immediately.
```

This leads to the three cash-flow categories.

---

## Operating cash flow

```text
Operating cash flow = cash generated by the normal business
```

This answers:

> Does the core business produce cash?

This is one of the most important financial-health concepts.

---

## Investing cash flow

```text
Investing cash flow = cash spent on or received from investments
```

Examples:

```text
buying factories
buying equipment
buying software infrastructure
selling assets
acquisitions
```

This is where capital expenditure appears.

```text
Capex = capital expenditure
```

---

## Financing cash flow

```text
Financing cash flow = cash from or to investors and lenders
```

Examples:

```text
issuing debt
repaying debt
issuing shares
buying back shares
paying dividends
```

This connects the business engine to the balance sheet.

---

## Free cash flow

After operating the business and maintaining/investing in assets, what cash remains?

```text
Free cash flow = operating cash flow - capital expenditure
```

This is a crucial concept.

Teaching point:

> Free cash flow is the cash the company can use for debt repayment, dividends, buybacks, acquisitions, or reinvestment.

This unlocks:

```text
free cash flow yield
dividend safety
buyback capacity
debt repayment ability
quality of earnings
```

Free cash flow is often more useful than net profit for mature businesses.

---

# 5. Margins: “How efficient is the machine?”

Margins are just profit expressed as a percentage of revenue.

This is where the ratio logic becomes powerful.

```text
Margin = profit / revenue
```

Teach margins as efficiency measures.

## Gross margin

```text
Gross margin = gross profit / revenue
```

Question:

> How much does the company keep after direct production costs?

Example:

```text
Revenue: €100
Direct costs: €60
Gross profit: €40

Gross margin = 40%
```

---

## Operating margin

```text
Operating margin = operating profit / revenue
```

Question:

> How much does the company keep after running the business?

This is often more useful than gross margin because it includes salaries, marketing, R&D, administration, etc.

---

## Net margin

```text
Net margin = net profit / revenue
```

Question:

> How much final profit does the company keep for each euro of sales?

---

## Free cash flow margin

```text
Free cash flow margin = free cash flow / revenue
```

Question:

> How much real cash does the company generate from each euro of sales?

This is extremely useful because it combines operating quality and cash conversion.

---

# 6. Reinvestment: “What does the machine do with the cash?”

Once the company generates cash, it has choices.

```text
Cash generated by the business can be used for:
1. reinvestment
2. debt repayment
3. dividends
4. buybacks
5. acquisitions
6. cash reserves
```

This is where the learner starts to understand capital allocation.

A company can use cash badly or well.

## Reinvestment

The company puts money back into the business.

Examples:

```text
new factories
new stores
software development
research and development
sales team expansion
marketing
new markets
```

Good reinvestment means:

> The company can put money back into the business and earn attractive returns on that money.

This naturally leads to:

```text
ROIC
ROE
growth
quality
compounders
```

The best teaching bridge is:

```text
Profitability tells you how good the machine is today.
Reinvestment tells you how much bigger or better the machine can become tomorrow.
```

---

# The business-engine teaching map

You can summarize Part 3 like this:

```text
Revenue
→ How much money comes in?

Costs
→ How much money leaves to produce and operate?

Profit
→ How much is left according to accounting?

Cash flow
→ How much real cash is generated?

Margins
→ How efficient is the machine?

Reinvestment
→ Can the machine use retained cash to grow?
```

Then the learner can derive many indicators naturally:

```text
Revenue growth
= Is the top line expanding?

Gross margin
= Is the product/service economically attractive?

Operating margin
= Is the business efficient after operating costs?

Net margin
= How much final profit remains?

Free cash flow
= How much real cash is left?

FCF margin
= How much cash per euro of revenue?

Dividend safety
= Is cash flow enough to support dividends?

ROIC
= Does reinvested capital produce attractive returns?
```
