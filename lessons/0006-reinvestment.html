Reinvestment: what does the machine do with the cash?

The choice that quietly decides long-term returns.

We ended last lesson with Alpha holding €170 of free cash flow and a menu of things it could do with it. This lesson is about that menu — and why the choice matters as much as the amount. It's the last stop on the engine. 

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

The CEO is the steward of your spare cash

Picture giving a trusted manager your yearly savings and asking them to do the smartest thing with it. That's exactly a CEO's job with free cash flow. The set of choices is called capital allocation, and there are six doors:1 

1. Reinvest in the business   (new factories, R&D, expansion)
2. Repay debt
3. Pay dividends              (cash handed to shareholders)
4. Buy back shares
5. Make acquisitions
6. Build a cash reserve

Doors 2–6 mostly return or store the cash. Door 1 — reinvestment — is the one that can make the machine bigger. So it deserves the sharpest question.

The one question behind every allocation choice

Litmus test

Will a euro reinvested earn a better return inside the business than I'd get if they just handed it back to me? If yes, reinvest. If no, return it (dividends or buybacks). Reinvesting at poor returns just to look bigger is how managers destroy value.

To answer that, we need a number for "the return the business earns on money put into it." That number is ROIC — return on invested capital.

ROIC — the interest rate the business earns on its own money

Don't memorise a formula; hold the intuition. ROIC asks: for every €1 of capital put into this business, how much profit does it spin off each year? 

ROIC ≈ operating profit (after tax) ÷ capital invested

A shop costs €1,000 to build and throws off €200 of
profit a year → ROIC = 200 / 1,000 = 20%

A 20% ROIC is like a savings account paying 20% interest — extraordinary. A 3% ROIC is a tired machine barely beating a bank deposit.

Why high ROIC + reinvestment = a "compounder"

Here's the magic the whole course has been building toward. If a business earns a high ROIC and can keep reinvesting at that rate, its profits don't just grow — they compound. Watch €100 reinvested each year: 

Reinvested at 20% ROIC          Reinvested at 3% ROIC
Year 0:   €100                  Year 0:   €100
Year 5:   €249                  Year 5:   €116
Year 10:  €619                  Year 10:  €134

Same starting €100. After ten years, the high-ROIC machine is worth nearly 5× the low-ROIC one — purely from the rate at which it reinvests. This is what people mean by a quality compounder: a business that can pour cash back into itself at high returns, year after year.2

So returning cash can be the smart move

The flip side, and a common misread: a mature company with no high-return places to reinvest is right to hand cash back via dividends and buybacks. The mistake isn't paying dividends — it's a low-ROIC company reinvesting heavily anyway, building an empire that earns 3% on your money. Good capital allocation fits the cash to the opportunity.

The bridge to remember

Profitability tells you how good the machine is today. Reinvestment tells you how much bigger or better it can become tomorrow. It's why two companies with identical profit today can be worth wildly different amounts — one can compound, the other can only coast.

Check yourself

From memory — several of these pull together the whole engine.

A shop costs €1,000 to build and earns €200 profit a year. Its ROIC is:

  • 20% — the profit divided by capital invested
  • €800 — the capital reduced by the year's profit
  • 5× — the capital invested divided by profit

ROIC ≈ profit ÷ invested capital = €200 ÷ €1,000 = 20%. It's the return the business earns on each euro put into it.

A mature firm has no high-return projects left. The best use of its cash is usually:

  • Return it to shareholders via dividends or buybacks
  • Reinvest it heavily to make the company look far bigger
  • Leave it idle in cash forever and never deploy it

With no high-ROIC opportunities, reinvesting destroys value. Returning the cash lets shareholders redeploy it themselves — that's good capital allocation.

Why can two firms with equal profit today be worth very different amounts?

  • One can reinvest at high returns and compound; one cannot
  • One must be reporting its profit in a stronger currency
  • Profit today fully determines a company's value forever

Value depends on the future, not just today. A high-ROIC business that can keep reinvesting compounds; a coasting one can't — so their futures, and values, diverge.

Free cash flow of €170 funds dividends and buybacks. Those two choices are examples of:

  • Returning cash to the company's shareholders
  • Reinvesting cash to expand the core business
  • Borrowing fresh cash from the company's lenders

Dividends and buybacks both hand cash back to owners — the "return" side of the capital-allocation menu, as opposed to reinvesting for growth.
