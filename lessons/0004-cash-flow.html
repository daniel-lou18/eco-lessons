Cash flow: did the machine actually produce cash?

The most important leap in the whole course.

We've climbed all the way down to net profit. Now comes the twist that separates people who can read a company from people who just read headlines: profit and cash are not the same thing. 

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

A feeling you may already know

Imagine you're a freelancer. In December you finish a big job and send a €10,000 invoice. You've earned €10,000 — on paper, December was a great month. But the client doesn't pay until March. For three months your bank account is empty while your "profit" looks fantastic.

That gap — between what you've earned and what's in the bank — is the entire idea of this lesson. Companies live in that gap constantly.

Profit is an opinion. Cash is a fact. Profit is what the accounting rules say you earned in a period. Cash flow is money that actually moved in or out of the bank.1

Your new litmus test

Whenever you meet a number on a financial statement, run this test:

Litmus test

Did real money change hands this period? If yes, it's cash flow. If it's only a promise (a sale not yet paid) or an accounting spreading (a cost smeared across years), it shapes profit but it is not this period's cash.

The two reasons profit and cash drift apart

There are exactly two mechanisms behind almost every gap. Meet them once and you'll spot them forever.

Reason 1 — Timing (you record the sale before the money arrives)

A company sells €100,000 of goods in December.
The customer pays in February.

→ Accounting counts the profit now (December).
→ The cash shows up later (February).

This is accrual accounting: revenue and costs are recorded when they're earned or incurred, not when cash moves.1 It's the freelancer's December invoice, scaled up.

Reason 2 — Spreading (the cash leaves all at once, the cost is smeared) 

A company buys a machine for €1,000,000 today.
It will be used for 10 years.

→ The cash (€1,000,000) leaves the bank immediately.
→ Accounting spreads the cost as ~€100,000/year for 10 years.

That yearly slice is called depreciation — an expense that lowers profit each year without any cash leaving (the cash already left, once, up front). Depreciation is the classic "accounting entry, not a cash event." Apply the litmus test: did money move this year because of depreciation? No. So it reduces profit but not this year's cash.

See it on Company Alpha

Here's why this matters with our running company. Alpha's net profit was €200. But the cash its business actually generated — operating cash flow — was €250: 

Net profit                        €200
+ Depreciation (non-cash expense)  €50   ← add back: no cash left
= Operating cash flow             €250

The €50 of depreciation was subtracted to get profit, but no cash went out for it this year — so we add it back to find the real cash. Alpha looks more cash-generative than its profit suggests. For other companies the gap runs the other way, which is exactly when you should worry.

Teaching point

A company can be profitable but cash-poor (booking sales nobody has paid for yet) or cash-rich but barely profitable. When profit and cash disagree, experienced readers trust the cash.1

Where cash comes from: three buckets

Every euro of cash moving through a company drops into one of three buckets. You can classify any cash movement by asking "where did this come from, or go to?" 

OPERATING   cash from running the normal business
            (customers pay; the company pays staff & suppliers)

INVESTING   cash for buying/selling long-term assets
            (buying a factory or equipment; an acquisition;
             selling off a building)

FINANCING   cash to/from investors and lenders
            (borrowing or repaying debt; issuing shares;
             paying dividends; buying back shares)

For Alpha: operating brought in +€250; it spent −€80 in the investing bucket on new equipment (that spending is called capex, capital expenditure); the financing bucket might be, say, −€100 if it repaid debt and paid a dividend (illustrative).

The star of the three is operating cash flow. It answers the single most important health question about a business: does the core operation actually produce cash on its own? A company that only stays afloat by borrowing (financing) or selling assets (investing) is telling you something.

Check yourself

From memory — and a couple reach back to earlier lessons on purpose.

A firm books a big sale in December; the customer pays in March. In December:

  • Profit rises now, but the cash arrives later
  • Cash rises now, but the profit arrives later
  • Both profit and the cash rise at the same time

Accrual accounting records the sale when earned (December), so profit rises immediately. The cash only moves when the customer actually pays (March).

Depreciation lowers a year's profit. How much cash does it move that year?

  • None — the cash already left when bought
  • The full amount of that year's depreciation slice
  • More cash than the company first paid upfront

Depreciation is a non-cash expense: it spreads a past cash outlay across years. No money moves this year, which is why we add it back to find operating cash flow.

A company pays a dividend to its shareholders. Which bucket is that cash in?

  • Financing — cash going back to the investors
  • Operating — cash from running the daily business
  • Investing — cash used to buy a long-term asset

Dividends, buybacks, and borrowing/repaying debt all sit in the financing bucket: cash flowing between the company and its investors and lenders.

When a company's profit and its cash flow disagree, the cautious reader trusts:

  • The cash — money that actually changed hands
  • The profit — the standard accounting bottom line
  • The revenue — the largest of the three figures

Profit is an opinion shaped by accounting rules; cash is a fact. Persistent gaps are a warning sign, so cash gets the benefit of the doubt.
