Margins: how efficient is the machine?

The same profits, turned into numbers you can compare.

You now know every profit layer and free cash flow. But there's a problem: raw euros don't let you compare a corner bakery with a giant, or tell whether a company is getting better. The fix is to turn each profit into a percentage of revenue — a margin. This is the last stop on the engine. 

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

A margin is just a savings rate

Two friends each say, "I saved €500 last month." Impressive? You can't tell until you ask what they earn. €500 on a €2,000 income is a 25% savings rate; €500 on €10,000 is only 5%. The percentage — not the euros — tells you who's efficient.

A company's margin is exactly that: its "savings rate" on sales.1 

Margin = profit ÷ revenue

And since we already have revenue as the base and every profit layer stacked on top, the margins just fall out — one for each layer.

Alpha's margin ladder

Watch the same €1,000 of revenue stepping down, with each cost layer biting a little more out of the percentage: 

Revenue              €1,000   = 100%
  ↳ Gross profit       €600   →  60%   gross margin
  ↳ Operating profit   €300   →  30%   operating margin
  ↳ Net profit         €200   →  20%   net margin
  ↳ Free cash flow     €170   →  17%   FCF margin

That descending staircase is the cost story from Lesson 2, now in percentages. Each step down is one more layer of cost. Read top to bottom and you can see exactly where a company's money goes.

What each rung tells you

Gross margin      is the PRODUCT itself attractive?
                  (60%: Alpha keeps 60c of every sales euro
                   after making the product)

Operating margin  is the BUSINESS run efficiently?
                  (the single most useful margin — it includes
                   staff, marketing, R&D, admin)

Net margin        what finally STICKS after interest & tax?

FCF margin        how much real CASH per euro of sales?
                  (combines efficiency + cash conversion)

The rule that stops most beginner mistakes

Teaching point

Compare margins within an industry, never across. A software firm with an 80% gross margin and a supermarket with a 25% gross margin can both be excellent — they're different machines (remember Lesson 3). Judging the supermarket against the software firm is like judging a fish on its climbing.

Two ways margins earn their keep:

1. ACROSS rivals (same industry):
   higher operating margin = the more efficient operator

2. OVER TIME (one company):
   rising margins → pricing power or improving efficiency
   falling margins → rising competition or cost pressure

A margin trend over five years often tells you more than any single year's number.

Check yourself

From memory — and notice these stitch the whole engine together.

A company has €1,000 revenue and €300 operating profit. Its operating margin is:

  • 30% — operating profit divided by revenue
  • €700 — revenue reduced by operating profit
  • 3.3× — the revenue divided by operating profit

Operating margin = operating profit ÷ revenue = €300 ÷ €1,000 = 30%. Every margin is just a profit layer over revenue.

A supermarket's 25% gross margin sits far below a software firm's 80%. This means:

  • Little — they are different kinds of business
  • The supermarket is clearly a failing, weak business
  • The software firm always earns more total profit

Gross margins differ hugely by industry. Compare within an industry, not across — a low-gross-margin retailer can be a fine business run on volume.

A company's operating margin has fallen for five straight years. A likely reading is:

  • Rising competition or cost pressure on the business
  • The company must have stopped selling any products
  • Revenue and profit are now exactly equal amounts

A steady margin decline usually signals tougher competition or rising costs eating efficiency. The trend often reveals more than one year's level.

Which margin best captures real cash kept per euro of sales?

  • FCF margin — free cash flow over the revenue
  • Gross margin — gross profit over the revenue
  • Net margin — net profit over the revenue

FCF margin = free cash flow ÷ revenue. Because it's built on cash (not accounting profit), it blends operating efficiency with cash conversion.
