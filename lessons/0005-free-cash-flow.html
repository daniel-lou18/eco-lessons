Free cash flow: what's left to actually do something with?

The cash a company is genuinely free to spend.

Last lesson we found Alpha's operating cash flow: €250 of real cash thrown off by the business. But not all of that is truly "spare" — some must be ploughed straight back in just to keep the machine running. What survives that is the prize: free cash flow.

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Free cash flow → Reinvestment

You already do this with your own paycheck

Your salary lands in your account — call that your operating cash flow. But some spending isn't really optional: you have to keep your car running to get to work, replace the laptop when it dies. Only what's left after the unavoidable "stay-earning" spending is money you can freely choose to save, invest, or enjoy.

A company faces the same split. The unavoidable spending is capex (capital expenditure) — the cash it lays out on long-term assets like machines, shops, or servers. Free cash flow is what remains after it.

The formula falls right out of last lesson

Free cash flow = operating cash flow − capex

Company Alpha:
  Operating cash flow   €250
  − Capex               −€80
  = Free cash flow      €170

Nothing to memorise: it's just the cash the business made, minus the cash it had to reinvest in its own assets to stay in business.

Teaching point — why "free"

It's called free because the company can do anything with it. Free cash flow is the pool that funds every one of these choices:1

reinvest for growth   repay debt   pay dividends
buy back shares       make acquisitions   build a cash reserve

That menu is the entire subject of the next lesson. Free cash flow is the money that makes those choices possible.

Why low — or negative — free cash flow isn't always bad

Here's where judgment beats a single number. Two companies can have the same €250 operating cash flow and tell completely opposite stories, depending on capex:

STEADY CO (mature brand)     GROW CO (building factories)
Operating cash flow €250     Operating cash flow €250
− Capex             −€30     − Capex            −€240
= Free cash flow    €220     = Free cash flow     €10

Steady Co barely needs to reinvest, so almost all its cash is free — lovely for paying dividends. Grow Co is pouring cash into new factories, leaving little free today. But that capex may be building tomorrow's earning power. Negative free cash flow from a young, growing company can be a sign of ambition; negative free cash flow from a mature one that should be coasting is a red flag. Same number, opposite meaning — so always ask why.

Useful nuance: capex comes in two flavours — maintenance capex (just to keep running) and growth capex (to expand). A company can't escape the first; the second is a choice.

The payoff: free cash flow yield

Just as net profit gave us P/E, free cash flow gives the owner's version of a return. Compare the cash to what the whole equity costs — Alpha's market value was €2,500:

FCF yield = free cash flow ÷ market cap
          = €170 ÷ €2,500
          = 6.8%

In plain words: for every €100 you pay for Alpha's shares, the business throws off about €6.80 of genuinely spare cash a year — like an interest rate the company pays you out of real money. It's P/E's cousin, but built on cash you can spend rather than accounting profit — which is why many investors trust it more, especially for mature businesses.1

Check yourself

From memory — some of these reach back across the cash-flow lessons.

A company has €250 operating cash flow and €80 of capex. Its free cash flow is:

  • €170 — the cash flow minus the capex
  • €330 — the cash flow plus the spent capex
  • €80 — only the capex the firm spent

Free cash flow = operating cash flow − capex = €250 − €80 = €170. It's the cash left after reinvesting to stay in business.

A young company building factories shows negative free cash flow. This is:

  • Possibly fine — heavy capex can fund future growth
  • Always a disaster — it proves the business is failing
  • Impossible — free cash flow can never be a minus

High growth capex can push FCF negative while building tomorrow's earning power. The same negative number from a mature firm would worry you far more — so ask why.

Capex (buying a new factory) lives in which cash-flow bucket?

  • Investing — cash spent on long-term assets
  • Operating — cash from the daily business itself
  • Financing — cash exchanged with the lenders

Buying long-term assets is the investing bucket from last lesson. Free cash flow deliberately nets that capex against operating cash flow.

Why do many investors trust free cash flow over net profit?

  • It is real cash, far harder to massage
  • It is always the largest number on the page
  • It completely ignores how much capex costs

Profit is an opinion shaped by accounting choices; free cash flow is cash that actually changed hands after reinvestment — much harder to dress up.
