The Business Engine · Lesson 0002 · ~7 min

Costs: what does the machine consume?

Money enters at the top — and starts leaving immediately.

Last time we opened the first valve, Revenue, and learned the load-bearing idea: revenue is not profit. Today we follow the money one step down the machine and watch costs carve that revenue away.

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

Costs come in layers, not one lump

The single most useful habit here is to stop thinking of "costs" as one big number. Costs peel off revenue in three layers, and each layer leaves behind a different kind of profit. This is the map for the whole income statement:1

Revenue
−  Direct costs        (layer 1)
=  Gross profit

Gross profit
−  Operating expenses  (layer 2)
=  Operating profit

Operating profit
−  Interest & taxes    (layer 3)
=  Net profit

You don't need to interpret each profit line yet — that's the next lesson. For now, just learn which costs sit on which layer.

Layer 1 — Direct costs (cost of goods sold)

"Cost of goods sold," usually shortened to COGS, is just a formal name for the costs tied directly to making or delivering each thing you sell. There's a simple test to spot one:2

The test: if you sold one more unit tomorrow, would this cost go up? If yes, it's a direct cost.

The obvious examples are physical, but a few catch people out — so here's what each actually means:

raw materials        the flour in the loaf; the steel in the car
manufacturing        the labour & machine time to build each unit
shipping             posting the finished product to the buyer
cloud computing      the extra server bill each new app user creates
payment processing   the ~2% a card network skims off every sale

Cloud computing and card fees feel like "tech overhead," but they pass the test: one more user, one more sale → the cost ticks up. So they belong on this layer, not the next.

Sell a product for €100 that costs €60 to make, and you keep €40 of gross profit — the money left over to pay for everything else the company does. That's the intuition for this layer: is the core product even worth making before we add the cost of running a company around it?

Layer 2 — Operating costs

These are the costs of running the business itself — the things you pay for even on a day you sell nothing. The mirror-image test gives them away:

The test: would this cost still land on your desk in a week where you sold zero units? If yes, it's usually operating.

salaries (head office)   the finance, HR & management teams
sales & marketing        ads, the sales force, brand campaigns
R&D                      designing next year's product
                         (R&D = research & development)
rent & software          the offices and the tools the firm runs on
administration           legal, accounting, the "back office"

Subtract these from gross profit and you get operating profit — what's left to pay lenders and the tax authority.

Teaching point

Two companies can have the same gross profit yet very different operating profit. Imagine two cafés that both buy and sell coffee at the same markup (same gross profit) — but one rents a cheap corner unit and the other a glossy high-street flagship with three managers. Same product economics; very different operating efficiency. That gap is what this layer exposes.

Layer 3 — Financial & tax costs

Two last bites are taken before any money reaches the owners:

interest    the "rent" paid on borrowed money (debt)
taxes       the government's cut of the remaining profit
one-offs    rare, non-repeating hits — e.g. a lawsuit
            payout or the cost of closing a factory

Interest is worth a beat: if a company borrows money, it pays a fee for using it, just as you'd pay rent for a flat you don't own. A company with lots of debt loses a real slice of profit here every year, before it earns a cent for its owners.

What survives all three layers is net profit — the accounting "bottom line," and the number most often meant when the news simply says a company "made a profit."

Company Alpha, layer by layer

We'll follow one fictional company, Alpha, through the whole course. Watch a single €1,000 of revenue get carved down:

Revenue              €1,000
−  Direct costs       −€400
=  Gross profit        €600

−  Operating expenses −€300
=  Operating profit    €300

−  Interest            −€50
−  Taxes               −€50
=  Net profit          €200

Same company, same period — yet there are three different "profit" numbers (€600, €300, €200) depending on how far down the machine you read. That is exactly why a headline like "profits fell" is almost meaningless until you ask which profit.

Teaching point

Profit is not one number. There are layers of profit, because there are layers of cost. Hold this thought — it's the doorway to the next lesson.

Check yourself

From memory — the effort of recall is what makes it stick.

For a phone maker, which of these is a direct cost?

  • The screens and chips built into each phone
  • The salary paid to the head-office finance team
  • The budget for the national advertising campaign

Screens and chips rise with every extra phone made — that's a direct cost (COGS). Finance salaries and advertising are operating costs: you pay them to run the company, not to build one more unit.

Alpha: revenue €1,000, direct costs €400. Gross profit is:

  • €600 — that is revenue minus direct costs
  • €400 — that is the direct costs counted alone
  • €1,400 — that is revenue plus direct costs added

Gross profit = revenue − direct costs = €1,000 − €400 = €600. It's the first profit layer, before operating costs are touched.

Why does the course say "profit is not one number"?

  • Costs peel off in layers, so profit does too
  • Revenue is double-counted across two reporting years
  • The tax rate changes the price charged to buyers

Each cost layer (direct, then operating, then interest & tax) leaves behind a different profit: gross, operating, then net. Three layers of cost, three layers of profit.

Two firms have equal gross profit but different net profit. Why?

  • They differ in operating, interest, or tax costs
  • They must have sold goods at two different prices
  • They always report in two different currency units

Equal gross profit means the product economics match. The gap opens lower down — in operating expenses, interest, or tax. This is exactly the efficiency difference the operating layer exposes.
