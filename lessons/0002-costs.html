The Business Engine · Lesson 0002 · ~14 min

Costs: what does the machine consume?

Money enters at the top — and starts leaving immediately.

Last time we opened the first valve, Revenue, and learned the load-bearing idea: revenue is not profit. Today we follow the money one step down the machine and watch costs carve that revenue away. 

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

By the end you'll be able to sort any cost two ways: where it sits in the machine, and how it behaves as the business grows or shrinks. Those turn out to be two different questions — and keeping them apart is the most powerful idea in the lesson.

Costs come in layers, not one lump

The single most useful habit here is to stop thinking of "costs" as one big number. Costs peel off revenue in three layers, and each layer leaves behind a different kind of profit. This is the map for the whole income statement:1 

Revenue
−  Direct costs        (layer 1)
=  Gross profit

Gross profit
−  Operating expenses  (layer 2)
=  Operating profit

Operating profit
−  Interest & taxes    (layer 3)
=  Net profit

You don't need to interpret each profit line yet — that's the next lesson. For now, just learn which costs sit on which layer.

Layer 1 — Direct costs (cost of goods sold)

"Cost of goods sold," usually shortened to COGS, is just a formal name for the costs tied directly to making or delivering each thing you sell. There's a simple test to spot one:2

The test: if you sold one more unit tomorrow, would this cost go up? If yes, it's a direct cost.

The obvious examples are physical, but a few catch people out — so here's what each actually means: 

raw materials        the flour in the loaf; the steel in the car
manufacturing        the labour & machine time to build each unit
shipping             posting the finished product to the buyer
cloud computing      the extra server bill each new app user creates
payment processing   the ~2% a card network skims off every sale

Cloud computing and card fees feel like "tech overhead," but they pass the test: one more user, one more sale → the cost ticks up. So they belong on this layer, not the next.

Sell a product for €100 that costs €60 to make, and you keep €40 of gross profit — the money left over to pay for everything else the company does. That's the intuition for this layer: is the core product even worth making before we add the cost of running a company around it?

Direct to what? The cost object

The "one more unit" test is a great beginner rule, but it hides a subtlety. A cost is never direct in the abstract — it is direct to something. The sharper definition is:3

A cost is direct if it can be clearly attached to the specific thing whose cost you're measuring. That "thing" has a name in accounting: the cost object — literally, what are we trying to measure the cost of?

Change the cost object and the same cost can switch sides. Watch the CEO's salary move: 

Cost object = ONE PHONE
  screen, chip, battery, assembly labour  → direct
  CEO salary                              → not attachable to one phone

Cost object = THE WHOLE COMPANY
  CEO salary, legal team, accounting      → direct cost of the company
  (they are simply company-level costs)

So when this course says "direct cost," the unspoken cost object is the product or service being sold. Screens, chips and assembly labour attach to a phone; the head-office finance team, HR and the CEO do not — they keep the company running, not the product being built. So they fall to the next layer.

Layer 2 — Operating costs

These are the costs of running the business itself — the things you pay for even on a day you sell nothing. The mirror-image test gives them away:

The test: would this cost still land on your desk in a week where you sold zero units? If yes, it's usually operating. 

salaries (head office)   the finance, HR & management teams
sales & marketing        ads, the sales force, brand campaigns
R&D                      designing next year's product
                         (R&D = research & development)
rent & software          the offices and the tools the firm runs on
administration           legal, accounting, the "back office"

Subtract these from gross profit and you get operating profit — what's left to pay lenders and the tax authority.

Teaching point

Two companies can have the same gross profit yet very different operating profit. Imagine two cafés that both buy and sell coffee at the same markup (same gross profit) — but one rents a cheap corner unit and the other a glossy high-street flagship with three managers. Same product economics; very different operating efficiency. That gap is what this layer exposes.

Layer 3 — Financial & tax costs

Two last bites are taken before any money reaches the owners:

interest    the "rent" paid on borrowed money (debt)
taxes       the government's cut of the remaining profit
one-offs    rare, non-repeating hits — e.g. a lawsuit
            payout or the cost of closing a factory

Interest is worth a beat: if a company borrows money, it pays a fee for using it, just as you'd pay rent for a flat you don't own. A company with lots of debt loses a real slice of profit here every year, before it earns a cent for its owners.

What survives all three layers is net profit — the accounting "bottom line," and the number most often meant when the news simply says a company "made a profit."

Same word, different machine: what counts as "direct"

Our examples so far have been a factory's — raw materials, machine time. But a direct cost does not always look like steel and screws. Every business has a "cost of making or delivering the thing it sells" — accountants call the non-manufacturing version cost of revenue — it just takes a different shape. Put three businesses side by side: 

                 COGS / cost of revenue          Operating expenses
                 (the product engine)            (the company engine)
───────────────  ──────────────────────────────  ────────────────────────
MANUFACTURER     materials, factory labour,      head office, marketing,
(phone maker)    factory overhead, machine        sales, R&D, finance,
                 depreciation, freight-out        HR, legal, management

RETAILER         the cost of buying the stock,    store rent, shop staff,
(supermarket)    freight-in, packaging            marketing, website, admin

SOFTWARE         hosting/servers, customer        product development,
(SaaS app)       support, payment fees, third-    sales & marketing,
                 party infrastructure             management, office, admin
Teaching point

A software company has no factory and no raw materials — yet it still has a cost of delivering each unit of service: the servers it runs on, the support it answers, the card fee on each subscription. "Direct cost" is a role in the business, not a physical object. Always ask what does it cost to serve one more customer? — the answer is this layer, whatever the industry.

Costs you can't pin on a single unit

Some direct costs are easy: one screen goes into one phone, full stop. But many costs are genuinely part of making the product, yet refuse to attach to any single unit: 

factory rent · factory electricity · machine maintenance
factory supervisor's salary · quality control

None of these belongs to "phone #38,204" — but you plainly can't make phones without them. So accounting allocates (spreads) them across all the units produced. This shared bucket has a name: manufacturing overhead.5 Spreading it is simple arithmetic: 

Factory rent for the year   €100,000
Phones produced               50,000
──────────────────────────  ────────
Allocated rent per phone        €2.00   ← each phone "absorbs" €2 of rent
Teaching point

This is why COGS is more than raw materials. Some costs aren't visible inside any one product, yet accounting smears them across the products anyway — so they ride along inside the direct-cost layer.

Depreciation: a cost paid once, charged slowly

A machine is the same story stretched over years. Buy it and you don't get to count €100,000 of cost in one go — the machine is consumed gradually as it wears out, so its cost is spread across its useful life. That yearly slice is called depreciation: 

Machine purchase price   €100,000
Useful life               10 years
───────────────────────  ────────
Depreciation per year     €10,000

And depreciation obeys the exact same "direct to what?" logic as every other cost — where the asset is used decides which layer its depreciation lands on: 

Factory machine     → depreciation rides inside COGS  (production cost)
Head-office laptop  → depreciation is an operating expense

Renting that machine instead would simply show up as a rental expense each year — no spreading needed. Buying it turns one cash outlay into ten years of depreciation. We'll see why that gap between "cash paid" and "cost charged" matters enormously when we reach Cash flow; for now, just note that even a machine follows the layer rule.

The second dimension: how a cost behaves

Everything so far answered one question: where does the cost sit? There's a second, completely separate question: what happens to the cost when sales rise or fall? Costs behave in three patterns:4 

Variable       moves closely with each sale
               raw materials · card fees · shipping per order · cloud per user

Fixed          exists even if sales are low
               office rent · salaried head-office staff · insurance · software

Semi-variable  flat for a while, then JUMPS when capacity must expand
               warehouse staff · support team · factory shifts · servers

Variable and fixed are intuitive. The one worth slowing down on is the third.

Semi-variable costs rise in steps, not smoothly. Picture a support team that comfortably handles 10,000 customers. From customer 1 to customer 10,000 the cost barely moves. But the 10,001st customer tips you over capacity — now you must hire a whole second team, and the cost jumps: 

Up to 10,000 customers  → one team        (cost flat)
The 10,001st customer   → hire team #2    (cost steps up)

Most real-world costs are not the tidy "one more sale → exactly €1 more" textbook line. They sit still, then leap. That step-shape is what makes scaling a business interesting — and it's why this second dimension exists at all.

Two dimensions, not one

Here is the trap almost every beginner falls into: assuming "direct" means the same as "variable," and "operating" means the same as "fixed." They don't. They are two independent dimensions, and a cost has a position on each: 

Direct vs operating   tells you WHERE the cost sits in the income statement
Fixed vs variable     tells you HOW the cost behaves as volume changes

Mix and match them and all four combinations exist:

COST                       WHERE it sits             HOW it behaves
─────────────────────────  ────────────────────────  ──────────────────────
Screen in each phone       Direct                    Variable
Card payment fee           Direct                    Variable
Factory worker's wage      Direct                    Semi-fixed
Factory supervisor         Direct (mfg overhead)     Fixed-ish
Head-office rent           Operating                 Fixed
Sales commission           Operating                 Variable
Advertising campaign       Operating                 Discretionary / semi-fixed

Read the surprising rows. A factory worker is a direct cost of making phones — yet she's paid monthly whether the line turns out 900 phones or 1,000, so her wage is not neatly variable. And a sales commission is an operating expense — yet it climbs with every sale, so it is variable. Direct ≠ variable; operating ≠ fixed.

Teaching point — the load-bearing idea

Costs have two separate dimensions: where they sit in the income statement, and how they behave when the business grows. Keep them apart and the rest of the course — margins, scaling, operating leverage, break-even — falls into place. Blur them and nothing later will quite make sense.

Company Alpha, layer by layer

We'll follow one fictional company, Alpha, through the whole course. Watch a single €1,000 of revenue get carved down: 

Revenue              €1,000
−  Direct costs       −€400
=  Gross profit        €600

−  Operating expenses −€300
=  Operating profit    €300

−  Interest            −€50
−  Taxes               −€50
=  Net profit          €200

Same company, same period — yet there are three different "profit" numbers (€600, €300, €200) depending on how far down the machine you read. That is exactly why a headline like "profits fell" is almost meaningless until you ask which profit.

What each layer is really telling you

The three layers aren't just bookkeeping bins. Each one answers a different question about the business — so each subtraction is a diagnostic: 

Revenue − Direct costs       → Is the PRODUCT worth making/delivering?
= Gross profit                 (the product engine)

Gross profit − Operating exp → Is the BUSINESS efficient to run?
= Operating profit             (the company engine)

Operating profit − Int/tax   → What's left after lenders & the government?
= Net profit                   (the capital structure)

Read it as three questions stacked on top of each other. Direct costs probe the product: is the thing even profitable to make? Operating costs probe the organisation: is the company expensive to run around that product? Interest probes the financing: is the company weighed down by debt? A business can pass one test and fail the next — which is the whole reason we read profit in layers rather than as a single number.

Teaching point

Profit is not one number. There are layers of profit, because there are layers of cost. Hold this thought — it's the doorway to the next lesson.

A pocket test for any cost. Run a cost through these and you've placed it on both dimensions at once: 

WHERE does it sit?
  1. Needed to MAKE or DELIVER the product?        → COGS / cost of revenue
  2. Needed to RUN, sell, manage, research, admin? → operating expense
  3. Tied to FINANCING the company (borrowed money)? → interest
  4. The government's share of the profit?           → tax

HOW does it behave?
  5. Does it move with volume? → variable, fixed, or semi-variable

Check yourself

From memory — the effort of recall is what makes it stick.

For a phone maker, which of these is a direct cost?

  • The screens and chips built into each phone
  • The salary paid to the head-office finance team
  • The budget for the national advertising campaign

Screens and chips attach to each phone (the cost object) — that's a direct cost (COGS). Finance salaries and advertising keep the company running, not the product being built, so they're operating costs.

A sales commission paid on every sale is best described as:

  • An operating expense that is also variable
  • A direct cost, because it rises with each sale
  • A fixed cost, because it's part of the sales team

Two dimensions, remember. Where it sits: selling the product is running the business → operating. How it behaves: it climbs with each sale → variable. Direct ≠ variable; operating ≠ fixed.

One support team serves up to 10,000 customers; number 10,001 forces a second team. That cost is:

  • Semi-variable — flat for a while, then it jumps
  • Purely variable — it rises by the same amount per customer
  • Purely fixed — it never changes with customer count

Semi-variable (step) costs stay flat across a capacity band, then leap when capacity must expand. Real costs rarely rise one smooth euro at a time.

Factory rent is €100,000 a year and 50,000 phones are made. Each phone's share of rent is:

  • €2, and it's spread into COGS as manufacturing overhead
  • €0 — rent is fixed, so it never touches the cost of a phone
  • €100,000 — the full rent is charged to every single phone

€100,000 ÷ 50,000 = €2 per phone. The rent can't attach to one unit, so it's allocated across all of them — which is why COGS is more than raw materials.

A €100,000 machine lasts 10 years. Where does its €10,000-a-year depreciation land?

  • In COGS if it's a factory machine; in operating expenses if it's head-office kit
  • Always in operating expenses, because depreciation isn't a real cost
  • All €100,000 hits the first year, since that's when it was bought

The asset is consumed gradually, so its cost is spread over its life (€100,000 ÷ 10 = €10,000/yr). And like any cost, where it's used decides the layer: factory → COGS, head office → operating.

"Revenue − direct costs = gross profit" mainly answers which question?

  • Is the product itself worth making or delivering?
  • Is the company carrying too much debt?
  • How much tax will the government take this year?

Gross profit probes the product engine. Debt shows up at the interest line, and tax at the very bottom — different layers, different questions.
