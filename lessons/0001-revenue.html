The Business Engine · Lesson 0001 · ~6 min

Revenue: how much money enters the machine?

Where every other number begins.

The mental model. A company is a machine: it takes money in, spends to operate, keeps some profit, turns some profit into cash, then decides what to do with that cash. This whole course follows that flow:

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

Today is the very first valve: Revenue.

What revenue is

Revenue is simply the money a company receives from selling its goods or services over a period of time.1 It sits at the top of the funnel, before a single cost is taken out.

Revenue = price × quantity sold

A company sells 1,000 products at €100 each.
Revenue = 1,000 × €100 = €100,000

The one distinction that matters today

Teaching point

Revenue is not profit. A company with huge revenue can still lose money. Revenue tells you the size and growth of a business — not whether it is efficient or profitable.

Picture a shop that sells €100,000 of goods but spent €130,000 buying and running everything. Revenue is large; the business still bled €30,000. Size is not the same as success. We will spend the next few lessons turning revenue into profit, one cost layer at a time.

The four questions to ask of any revenue line

Once you can read a revenue number, the useful work is interrogating its quality. Four questions do most of the job:2

Growing?              is this year's revenue bigger than last year's?
Recurring or one-off? does it repeat automatically (a Netflix
                      subscription) or must it be re-won each time
                      (a builder who finishes a house and moves on)?
Diversified or not?   spread across many customers, or dangerously
                      dependent on one or two big ones?
Cyclical or stable?   does it swing with the economy (a carmaker in
                      a recession) or hold steady (a utility, bread)?

Two companies can report the same €100m of revenue — one from thousands of recurring subscriptions, one from a single contract that ends next year. The number is identical; the businesses are not. "Cyclical" just means the revenue rises and falls with the broader economy's cycle.

Why revenue is the base of everything

Revenue is the denominator for a surprising number of concepts you will meet later. Keep this list in the corner of your eye — you do not need it yet, but it shows why we start here:

revenue growth
price / sales ratio
gross margin          = gross profit / revenue
operating margin      = operating profit / revenue
net margin            = net profit / revenue
free cash flow margin = free cash flow / revenue

Every one of those is "something ÷ revenue." Master the denominator first.

Which statement about revenue is correct?

  • A company can have large revenue and still lose money
  • A company with large revenue is always a profitable business
  • A company with large revenue is always holding lots of cash

Revenue is the top of the funnel — measured before any cost is subtracted. Size is not the same as profit or cash.

A bakery sells 4,000 loaves at €3 each. Its revenue is:

  • €12,000 — that is four thousand times three
  • €1,333 — that is the four thousand over three
  • €4,003 — that is the four thousand plus three

Revenue = price × quantity = €3 × 4,000 = €12,000. Nothing is deducted yet; costs come in the next lesson.

Which revenue stream is the most durable?

  • Monthly subscriptions paid by thousands of customers
  • A single large one-off contract ending early next year
  • A surge from one product going briefly viral online

Recurring, diversified revenue is far more durable than a one-off or concentrated source — even when the headline number is identical.
