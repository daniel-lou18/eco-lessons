Profit: what does the machine keep?

Three numbers wear the name "profit." Learn to ask which.

In Costs we watched three layers of cost peel away from revenue, each leaving a different profit behind. Today we stop calculating those numbers and start reading them — what each one actually tells you about a business. 

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

You already know this from your payslip

Before any finance, an everyday example you've lived. Look at a payslip and you see two big numbers: gross pay (the headline salary) and net pay (what actually lands in your account after tax and deductions). Same money, measured at two points, with bites taken in between.

A company's income statement is the exact same idea — it just has three checkpoints instead of two, because it pays its costs in three rounds: 

Gross profit      what's left after making the product
Operating profit  what's left after running the business
Net profit        what's truly left for the owners
Teaching point

"Profit" is not one number. So when a headline says "profits fell," your first reflex should be a question: which profit — gross, operating, or net? They can move in opposite directions, and each points at a different part of the machine.

Gross profit — "is the product itself worth making?"

Gross profit is revenue minus the direct cost of the product. It tells you about the product's own economics, before any of the cost of running a company. The fastest way to feel what it reveals is to put two very different businesses side by side. 

SOFTWARE app          SUPERMARKET
Sells a €100 sub      Sells €100 of groceries
Costs ~€5 to deliver  Bought them for €75
(servers)             (from suppliers)
─────────────         ─────────────
Gross profit €95      Gross profit €25
keeps €95 per €100    keeps €25 per €100

Same €100 of revenue, wildly different gross profit. The software firm keeps almost everything from each new customer, so it has huge room to spend on growth. The supermarket keeps little per sale, so it lives or dies on volume. Neither is "better" — but gross profit is the first place that difference becomes visible.1

This is why comparing a software company's profit to a supermarket's, euro for euro, is almost meaningless. Different machines.

Operating profit — "is the business worth running?"

Take gross profit and subtract the cost of running the company (the operating costs from last lesson). What's left is operating profit — and it's the number professional analysts watch most closely. Why? Because it captures how well the company runs its actual operations, before the distractions of how it's financed (debt) or taxed.

Remember the two cafés from last lesson — same coffee, same markup, but one in a cheap corner unit and one in a glossy flagship with three managers? They have identical gross profit. The difference only shows up here, at the operating line. Operating profit is where good and bad management becomes visible.

Net profit — "what's left for the owners?"

Subtract the last bites — interest and tax — and you reach net profit (also called net income, or "the bottom line," because it literally sits at the bottom of the statement). This is the number the news usually means when it just says a company "made a profit."

Teaching point

Net profit is the most quoted number — but also the most massageable. A one-off event (selling a building, a legal settlement) can swell or shrink it for a single year, and accounting choices can nudge it. We'll see in the next lesson why cash is the harder-to-fake reality check.2

The payoff: where the news gets "P/E"

Here's the reward for climbing down the machine. That headline number you've seen a thousand times — the P/E ratio — isn't a formula to memorise. It falls out of net profit in two small steps. Let's use our running company, Alpha (net profit €200), and say it has been split into 100 shares, trading at €25 each. 

Step 1 — slice the profit per share (EPS):
  EPS = net profit ÷ shares = €200 ÷ 100 = €2 per share
  (EPS = "earnings per share" — each share's slice of net profit)

Step 2 — compare price to that slice (P/E):
  P/E = price ÷ EPS = €25 ÷ €2 = 12.5

What 12.5 means in plain words: you're paying €12.50 for every €1 the company earns in a year. Put differently — if profit never changed, it would take about 12.5 years of earnings to add back up to the price you paid. A high P/E means the market expects earnings to grow; a low one often means it expects trouble.

So "P/E" is just price measured in years-of-profit. You derived it from the engine instead of memorising it — that's the whole game in this course.

Check yourself

From memory — and notice a couple of these reach back to earlier lessons. Mixing topics on purpose is what builds lasting recall.

A news headline says "profit dropped." What should you ask first?

  • Which profit — gross, operating, or net — fell?
  • How many total products did the company manage to sell?
  • Which currency was used to report the yearly figure?

"Profit" names three different numbers. Gross, operating, and net can even move in opposite directions, so the layer matters before the direction does.

A software app and a supermarket each take €100 of revenue. Which keeps more gross profit, and why?

  • Software — its direct cost per sale is tiny
  • Supermarket — it always sells a far higher volume
  • Neither — equal revenue must mean equal profit

Gross profit = revenue − direct cost. The app spends ~€5 to serve a €100 sale; the supermarket spent €75 buying the goods. Same revenue, very different gross profit.

Two cafés have identical gross profit but different operating profit. The cause sits in:

  • Operating costs, such as rent and extra managers
  • Direct costs, such as the coffee beans and the milk
  • The selling price of a single cup of the coffee

Equal gross profit means matching product economics (price and direct cost). The gap can only open at the next layer down — operating costs like rent and staff.

A company earns €2 EPS and its share trades at €30. Its P/E is:

  • 15 — that is the price divided by EPS
  • €60 — that is the price multiplied by EPS
  • €28 — that is the price reduced by the EPS

P/E = price ÷ EPS = €30 ÷ €2 = 15. You're paying 15× one year's earnings per share.
