The Business Engine · Lesson 0003 · ~18 min

Profit: what does the machine keep?

Three numbers wear the name "profit." Each is a checkpoint on the machine — learn to read which one moved, and why.

In Costs we watched three layers of cost peel away from revenue, each leaving a different profit behind. Today we stop calculating those numbers and start reading them. 

Revenue → Costs → Profit → Cash flow → Margins → Reinvestment

The one idea that upgrades everything else in this lesson: each profit number is not just a calculation — it is a diagnostic checkpoint. Each one tests a different part of the engine. By the end you'll never again hear "profits fell" without instantly asking which profit, and why?

You already know this from your payslip

Before any finance, an everyday example you've lived. Look at a payslip and you see two big numbers: gross pay (the headline salary) and net pay (what actually lands in your account after tax and deductions). Same money, measured at two points, with bites taken in between.

A company's income statement is the exact same idea — it just has three checkpoints instead of two, because it pays its costs in three rounds: 

Gross profit      what's left after making the product
Operating profit  what's left after running the business
Net profit        what's truly left for the owners

The upgrade: each layer is a different diagnosis

Last lesson taught you that profit comes in layers. Here's the deeper truth: each layer isolates a different part of the machine. So instead of reading the three numbers as "how much money is left," read each as a question the business has to pass: 

Gross profit     → Is the product/service economically attractive?
Operating profit → Is the business model efficient to run?
Net profit       → What's left after financing, taxes & accounting noise?

Why does this matter so much? Because "profit fell" on its own tells you almost nothing. The same drop means three completely different things depending on where it happened: 

Lower gross profit      → the product became less attractive economically
Lower operating profit  → the business became more expensive to run
Lower net profit        → debt, taxes, or a one-off hit the bottom line
Teaching point — the load-bearing idea

The beginner asks: did profit go up or down? The skilled reader asks: where did profit move? A company can have a brilliant product (strong gross profit) and still be bleeding because it's bloated to run (weak operating profit), or drowning in debt (weak net profit). The layer tells you which part of the machine to inspect.

Gross profit — "is the product itself worth making?"

Gross profit is revenue minus the direct cost of the product. It tells you about the product's own economics, before any of the cost of running a company. The fastest way to feel what it reveals is to put two very different businesses side by side. 

SOFTWARE app          SUPERMARKET
Sells a €100 sub      Sells €100 of groceries
Costs ~€5 to deliver  Bought them for €75
(servers)             (from suppliers)
─────────────         ─────────────
Gross profit €95      Gross profit €25
keeps €95 per €100    keeps €25 per €100

Same €100 of revenue, wildly different gross profit. Think of gross profit as economic oxygen: it's the money the product hands the company before the rest of the business starts breathing. The software firm keeps €95 of every €100 — huge room to spend on sales, marketing, R&D, and support. The supermarket keeps only €25, so it has almost no room for error.1

That doesn't make software "better." It means a low-gross-profit business must compensate with relentless efficiency — high volume, fast inventory turnover, tight cost control, low waste, lean logistics. Neither is superior; gross profit just tells you how much room the product leaves for everything else.

This is why comparing a software company's profit to a supermarket's, euro for euro, is almost meaningless. Different machines, different amounts of oxygen.

Gross profit can fall even when revenue rises

Here's where most people misread the number. Revenue and gross profit are not the same lever — gross profit can drop while sales climb: 

                 Year 1      Year 2
Revenue          €1,000      €1,200   ↑ sales grew
Direct costs      −€400       −€700
─────────────    ──────      ──────
Gross profit       €600        €500   ↓ but gross profit shrank

More was sold, yet the product kept less. Any of these could be why:

input costs rose · the firm had to discount prices · shipping got
dearer · production became less efficient · the PRODUCT MIX worsened

That last one — product mix — is the subtle one, so let's make it concrete. Suppose a company sells two products at the same €100 price but very different direct costs: 

PREMIUM product       BASIC product
Price        €100     Price        €100
Direct cost   −€40    Direct cost   −€80
────────────  ────    ────────────  ────
Gross profit  €60     Gross profit  €20

If customers shift toward the basic product, total revenue can look flat or even rise — yet gross profit quietly weakens, because each euro of sales now comes with more cost attached.

Teaching point

Gross profit is shaped not only by how much a company sells, but by what it sells and at what cost. So when gross profit moves, "we sold more / less" is rarely the whole story — ask about pricing, input costs, efficiency, and mix.

Operating profit — "is the business worth running?"

Take gross profit and subtract the cost of running the company (the operating costs from last lesson). What's left is operating profit — and it's the number professional analysts watch most closely. Why? Because it captures how well the company runs its actual operations, before the distractions of how it's financed (debt) or taxed.

Remember the two cafés from last lesson — same coffee, same markup, but one in a cheap corner unit and one in a glossy flagship with three managers? They have identical gross profit. The difference only shows up here. Made general, that's the whole point of this layer: 

                 Company A     Company B
Gross profit       €600          €600    ← identical product economics
Operating exp     −€300         −€550
─────────────     ─────         ─────
Operating profit   €300           €50    ← wildly different efficiency

Same product, same gross profit — but Company B keeps a sixth as much. Something about how it's run is eating the profit: too many staff, expensive offices, bloated management, inefficient marketing, weak pricing power. Operating profit is where good and bad management becomes visible.

Teaching point

But high operating expenses are not automatically bad. A fast-growing software firm may pour money into sales teams, marketing, R&D, and customer acquisition — depressing operating profit today to buy growth tomorrow. So the real question isn't "are operating costs high?" but "are they wasteful or strategic?" That distinction matters enormously — we return to it under profit quality below.

Operating leverage: the hidden amplifier

This is the most powerful idea in the lesson, and it lives right here at the operating line. Operating leverage means a company carries large fixed operating costs — so once revenue grows enough, extra gross profit falls almost straight through into operating profit, because the costs don't grow at the same speed.2 Watch a software firm double its revenue: 

                  Before       After (revenue ×2)
Revenue           €1,000       €2,000
Direct costs       −€100        −€200
─────────────     ──────       ──────
Gross profit        €900       €1,800
Operating exp      −€800        −€900   ← barely moved
─────────────     ──────       ──────
Operating profit    €100         €900   ← 9× bigger!

Revenue merely doubled. Operating profit grew ninefold. The company already had the product, the engineers, the office, the platform — so when more revenue arrived, costs barely rose to meet it. This is why some businesses look unremarkable when small and become extraordinarily profitable at scale.

But leverage is a two-way amplifier. The same fixed costs that magnify profits on the way up will magnify losses on the way down: 

High fixed costs + RISING revenue   → profit can explode upward
High fixed costs + FALLING revenue  → profit can collapse downward

This is precisely why operating profit is more sensitive than gross profit. Gross profit moves roughly in step with sales; operating profit, sitting on top of a fixed-cost base, swings much harder in both directions.

Net profit — "what's left for the owners?"

Subtract the last bites — interest and tax — and you reach net profit (also called net income, or "the bottom line," because it literally sits at the bottom of the statement). This is the number the news usually means when it just says a company "made a profit."

The cleanest way to see what net profit adds is to compare two companies whose operations are equally good but whose financing differs: 

                  Company A     Company B
Operating profit    €300          €300    ← operations equally profitable
Interest            −€20         −€120    ← B is loaded with debt
Taxes               −€70          −€45
─────────────      ─────         ─────
Net profit          €210          €135

Identical business performance, very different bottom line — purely because Company B carries more debt. That gap is exactly why analysts prize operating profit: 

Operating profit  → how good is the business BEFORE financing?
Net profit        → what's left AFTER financing & taxes?

Operating profit separates business performance from capital structure (how the company is funded). Net profit folds them back together — useful, but it mixes "is the business good?" with "how is it financed?" into one number.

Reported profit vs normalised profit

Net profit has one more wrinkle worth meeting gently now. The reported net profit is the official accounting number — but a single year can contain unusual, one-off events that don't reflect the normal business. Investors mentally strip those out to estimate normalised profit: what the company would earn in an ordinary year.3 

A good one-off                  A bad one-off
Normal net profit  €200         Normal net profit  €200
+ sold a building  +€100        − legal settlement −€100
─────────────────  ────         ─────────────────  ────
Reported profit    €300         Reported profit    €100

Did the first business suddenly get better? No — the extra €100 came from selling a building, not from serving customers. Did the second get worse? Maybe not, if the settlement is genuinely a one-time hit. So: 

Reported profit    → what the accounting statement says this year
Normalised profit  → what the business might earn in a NORMAL year

Don't over-engineer this yet — it points toward valuation, which comes later. The habit to keep is small: when a profit number jumps or dives, ask "is this the normal business, or a one-off?"

Profit growth has quality, not just direction

Putting the last few ideas together gives you a genuinely expert habit. Net profit can rise for very different reasons — and they are not equally impressive: 

HEALTHY sources                 LOW-QUALITY sources
more revenue                    a temporary tax benefit
better pricing power            a one-off asset sale
lower direct costs              cutting essential R&D
better operating efficiency     underinvesting in maintenance
economies of scale              financial engineering

The trap is real. Imagine a company that slashes R&D from €200 to €50. Operating profit instantly rises by €150 — the chart looks great. But it may be starving its own future, gutting the product pipeline to flatter this year's number.

Teaching point

Higher profit is not automatically better. Always ask what created it. Profit that comes from a stronger product or a leaner operation is worth far more than profit conjured by a one-off sale or by skipping investment the business will miss later. This is the thread we pick up in Cash flow and Reinvestment.

Absolute profit vs margin: you can't compare euros alone

One quick but essential idea before the famous ratios. So far we've talked in euros kept. But euros alone can't be compared across companies of different sizes. Look: 

                 Company A      Company B
Revenue          €1,000        €10,000
Net profit         €100           €300
─────────────    ──────        ───────
Net margin          10%             3%      ← profit ÷ revenue

Company B keeps more total euros (€300 vs €100). But Company A is far more profitable for its size — it keeps 10 cents per euro of sales versus B's 3. That's the difference between two questions: 

Absolute profit  → how many euros did the company keep?
Margin           → how many cents per euro of revenue did it keep?

We give margins a whole lesson later (0007 · Margins). For now just hold the bridge: profit amounts are hard to compare without scale, and margin = profit ÷ revenue is how you put two different-sized companies on the same footing.

The payoff: from net profit to "EPS" and "P/E"

Here's the reward for climbing down the machine. The two numbers you've seen a thousand times in the news — EPS and the P/E ratio — aren't formulas to memorise. They fall out of net profit in small steps. Let's use our running company, Alpha (net profit €200), split into 100 shares, trading at €25 each.

Step 1 — slice the profit per share (EPS)

A company's total net profit belongs to all shareholders together. But you don't own the whole company — you own shares. So EPS ("earnings per share") simply translates company-level profit into the slice sitting behind one share: 

EPS = net profit ÷ shares = €200 ÷ 100 = €2 per share

Each Alpha share represents €2 of annual accounting earnings. Now the important twist — EPS depends on two things, not one: the profit and the number of shares. A company can grow its total profit and still deliver less per share if it issues lots of new shares: 

                  Year 1      Year 2
Net profit          €200        €300   ↑ total profit grew
Shares               100         200   ↑ but shares doubled
─────────────       ────        ────
EPS                   €2       €1.50   ↓ each share got less

Total profit rose, profit per share fell. That matters because owners hold shares, not abstract company profit — it's the seed of "dilution," which we'll meet properly in the balance-sheet module.

Basic vs diluted EPS — a light touch. You'll often see two EPS figures. Basic EPS divides by today's shares. Diluted EPS divides by today's shares plus shares that could come into existence later (from stock options, convertible bonds, employee share awards, warrants). You don't need the mechanics yet — just the instinct: 

Diluted EPS asks: "what if more shares appear?" → it's the safer,
more conservative number to use when comparing companies.

Step 2 — compare price to that slice (P/E)

Now put the share price next to its earnings slice:

P/E = price ÷ EPS = €25 ÷ €2 = 12.5

What 12.5 means in plain words: you're paying €12.50 for every €1 the company earns in a year. Put differently — if profit never changed, it would take about 12.5 years of earnings to add back up to the price you paid. So "P/E" is just price measured in years-of-profit. You derived it from the engine instead of memorising it — that's the whole game in this course.

Why P/E can mislead: it's a question, not a verdict

A low P/E looks "cheap" and a high P/E looks "expensive" — but both can be traps, because P/E sits on top of an earnings number that may be unreliable. Three warnings turn P/E from a verdict into a question generator:4 

WARNING 1 — earnings quality
A one-off gain inflates EPS, so P/E looks artificially LOW.
  Price €30, normal EPS €2  → normal P/E 15
  EPS jumps to €3 (one-off) → P/E "10"  ← looks cheaper, isn't

WARNING 2 — a low P/E can be a warning, not a bargain
  Price €20, this year's EPS €4 → P/E 5  ← looks cheap
  if next year's EPS falls to €1 → real P/E 20
Low P/E can mean: undervalued · OR peak/cyclical earnings ·
  declining business · high debt risk · a temporary profit boost

WARNING 3 — a high P/E can reflect growth, not waste
  Price €60, EPS €2 → P/E 30  ← looks expensive
  if EPS grows to €6 in a few years → the price looks reasonable
Teaching point

P/E is not a verdict. It is a question generator. A number you can't interpret in one glance — instead it should make you ask: are earnings normal? growing? cyclical? high-quality? Is the company heavily indebted? Is the market pricing in growth or decline? The ratio's job is to start the investigation, not end it.

The "which profit?" decision tree

Here's the practical tool that ties the whole lesson together — the way a mechanic reads an engine. Whenever you hear that profit moved, don't react to the direction. Trace it to a layer, then ask why: 

"PROFIT IMPROVED" — improved WHERE?
  Gross profit up?      → pricing, mix, input costs, or production improved
  Operating profit up?  → the firm scaled, cut overhead, or ran leaner
  Net profit up?        → maybe operations… or maybe interest/tax/one-offs

"PROFIT FELL" — fell WHERE?
  Gross profit down?      → product economics worsened
  Operating profit down?  → costs grew faster than gross profit
  Net profit down?        → financing, taxes, or a one-off charge

The whole lesson in five lines. 

Gross profit     → is the product economically attractive?
Operating profit → is the company efficient to run?
Net profit       → what's left for owners after financing, tax & noise?
EPS              → net profit sliced per share
P/E              → the share price measured in years-of-profit

And the one expert habit: never say "profit went up" or "profit went down" without asking — which profit, and why?

Check yourself

From memory — and notice a couple of these reach back to earlier lessons. Mixing topics on purpose is what builds lasting recall.

A news headline says "profit dropped." What should you ask first?

  • Which profit — gross, operating, or net — fell?
  • How many total products did the company manage to sell?
  • Which currency was used to report the yearly figure?

"Profit" names three different checkpoints. Gross, operating, and net can even move in opposite directions, so the layer matters before the direction does.

A company's revenue rises from €1,000 to €1,200, yet gross profit falls from €600 to €500. The most likely cause is:

  • Higher input costs, discounts, or a worse product mix
  • The company simply sold fewer units this year overall
  • Interest payments on the company's debt increased

Gross profit depends on what you sell and at what cost, not just how much. Rising revenue with falling gross profit points to costlier inputs, discounting, or a shift toward lower-margin products. Interest sits two layers down — it can't touch gross profit.

Two companies have identical gross profit but very different operating profit. The cause sits in:

  • Operating costs — staff, offices, overhead, marketing
  • Direct costs, such as the raw materials in the product
  • The selling price charged for a single unit

Equal gross profit means matching product economics (price and direct cost). The gap can only open at the next layer down — how efficiently the company is run.

A software firm doubles its revenue and its operating profit jumps ninefold. This is mainly because:

  • Its costs are largely fixed, so extra sales fall through (operating leverage)
  • It must have raised its prices by roughly nine times
  • Direct costs always shrink to zero once revenue grows

Operating leverage: with a big fixed-cost base already paid for, extra gross profit drops almost straight into operating profit. It works in reverse too — falling revenue would collapse profit just as sharply.

Two companies report the same operating profit but different net profit. The most likely reason is:

  • One carries more debt, so it pays more interest
  • One sells a completely different product to the other
  • One has a higher gross profit on each unit it sells

Equal operating profit means the operations are equally good. The gap opens below that line — at interest and tax. More debt means more interest, which is why analysts prize operating profit: it strips out capital structure.

A company's net profit rises only because it sold an old building this year. A careful reader concludes:

  • The underlying business may not be more profitable at all
  • The business has clearly become more efficient to run
  • Its gross profit on each product must have improved

That's a one-off, not normal operations. Reported profit went up, but normalised profit — what the business earns in an ordinary year — did not. Always ask what created the profit.

Company A earns €100 net on €1,000 revenue; Company B earns €300 net on €10,000 revenue. Which is more profitable for its size?

  • Company A — its net margin is 10% versus B's 3%
  • Company B — it keeps far more total euros of profit
  • Neither — total profit is the only fair comparison

Absolute profit favours B (€300 > €100), but margin (profit ÷ revenue) puts them on the same footing: A keeps 10 cents per euro of sales, B only 3. Euros alone can't be compared across sizes.

A company grows total net profit from €200 to €300 but its EPS falls. What happened?

  • It issued many new shares, so each share's slice shrank
  • This is impossible — more profit always means higher EPS
  • Its share price must have dropped during the year

EPS = net profit ÷ shares. If the share count rises faster than profit, the per-share slice falls even as total profit grows. Owners hold shares, not abstract company profit.

A company earns €2 EPS and its share trades at €30. Its P/E is:

  • 15 — that is the price divided by EPS
  • €60 — that is the price multiplied by EPS
  • €28 — that is the price reduced by the EPS

P/E = price ÷ EPS = €30 ÷ €2 = 15. You're paying 15× one year's earnings per share.

A stock has a very low P/E of 5. The most useful conclusion is:

  • It might be cheap — or the market expects earnings to fall
  • It is definitely undervalued and a guaranteed bargain
  • It is definitely overvalued and certain to disappoint

P/E is a question generator, not a verdict. A low P/E can mean a bargain — or peak/cyclical earnings, a declining business, or debt risk. It tells you to investigate, not to conclude.
