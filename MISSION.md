# Mission: Understand the company behind the headline

## Why
I already understand how stock *prices* move. Now I want to understand the
*business* underneath the ticker — so that when a company shows up in the news
("record revenue but the stock fell," "burning cash," "loaded with debt"), I
actually know what those words mean and can judge whether the story makes sense.
This is financial literacy, not a plan to start picking stocks.

## Success looks like
- Read a company's headline numbers and trace the **business engine**:
  revenue → costs → profit → cash flow → margins → reinvestment.
- Explain, in plain language, *why* a company can be profitable yet short of
  cash (and vice versa) — and why a journalist might highlight one or the other.
- Compute and interpret the core margins (gross, operating, net, FCF) and say
  what each one reveals about a business.
- Understand valuation ratios (P/E, P/S, FCF yield, EV/EBITDA) when they appear
  in articles — derived from the engine, not memorised as formulas.
- Combine the engine (earning power) with the balance sheet (financial
  structure) to place a company in one of the four quadrants — e.g. tell a
  "quality compounder" from a "danger zone" business.

## Constraints
- Self-taught, in short sessions. Lessons must be short and completable in one
  sitting.
- Prefers the **"company as a machine"** mental model used in the source
  outline — intuitive first, abstract later.
- Currency examples in euros (€), matching the source material.
- Learning by deriving concepts from first principles, not rote formula recall.
- Worked examples use the fictional **"Company Alpha"** from the outline until
  the basics are solid (not real filings yet).
- **Solo learning** — no community/forum involvement for now.

## Out of scope (for now)
- Picking or valuing individual stocks for a real portfolio (literacy first;
  this can become the mission later if the goal shifts).
- Building full DCF / valuation models in a spreadsheet.
- Accounting mechanics for their own sake (debits/credits, journal entries).
- Macro, technical analysis, options, and trading strategy.
- The balance-sheet module (Part 4) comes *after* the business engine (Part 3);
  it is sequenced next, not abandoned.
