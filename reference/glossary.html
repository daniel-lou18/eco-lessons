Glossary of the Business Engine

The compressed vocabulary of fundamental analysis, in the order money flows through the machine.

One company is a machine: it takes money in, spends to operate, keeps some profit, converts some profit to cash, then decides what to do with the cash. Every term below is one part of that machine.

Flow — what happens over a period

Revenue = price × quantity sold
Money received from selling goods or services. The top of the funnel. Not profit — a company with huge revenue can still lose money.
Cost of goods sold (COGS) / Direct costs
Costs tied directly to producing or delivering the product: raw materials, manufacturing, shipping, cloud infrastructure, payment processing, direct labour.
Operating expenses (OpEx)
Costs of running the business: salaries, sales & marketing, R&D, rent, software, administration, support.
Gross profit = revenue − direct costs
What remains after the cost of making the product. Answers: is the core product economically attractive?
Operating profit (EBIT) = gross profit − operating expenses
Profit from running the business, before interest and tax. Often more meaningful than gross profit, and the cleanest view of the business before capital structure (debt) and tax.
Operating leverage
The effect of having large fixed operating costs: once revenue grows past them, extra gross profit falls almost straight into operating profit — so profit swings harder than revenue, up and down. Makes operating profit more sensitive than gross profit.
Net profit (net income) = profit after all costs, interest & tax
The accounting "bottom line" — what's left for shareholders by the accounting rules. Standardised and comparable, but mixes business performance with financing and one-off items.
Reported vs normalised profit
Reported profit is the official figure for the year; normalised profit strips out one-off, non-recurring items (asset sales, legal settlements) to estimate what the business earns in a normal year. Ask of any profit jump: normal business, or a one-off?
EBITDA
Earnings before interest, tax, depreciation & amortisation. An operating-profit proxy that strips out non-cash charges. Useful but not cash flow.
EPS (earnings per share) = net profit ÷ shares
Net profit allocated to each share. Depends on both profit and share count — total profit can rise while EPS falls if new shares are issued. Feeds directly into P/E.
Basic vs diluted EPS
Basic EPS divides by today's shares; diluted EPS also counts shares that could appear later (options, convertibles, warrants, employee awards). Diluted is the more conservative number for comparing companies.
Operating cash flow
Cash actually generated by the normal business. Answers: does the core business produce cash?
Investing cash flow
Cash spent on / received from investments — buying factories, equipment, acquisitions; selling assets. Where capex lives.
Financing cash flow
Cash to/from investors and lenders: issuing or repaying debt, issuing or buying back shares, paying dividends.
Capex (capital expenditure)
Cash spent acquiring or maintaining long-term assets.
Free cash flow (FCF) = operating cash flow − capex
Cash left after operating and investing to maintain the business — available for debt repayment, dividends, buybacks, acquisitions, or reinvestment. Often more useful than net profit for mature businesses.

Cost classification — where a cost sits & how it behaves

Cost object
The thing whose cost you're measuring (one product, a customer, the whole company). A cost is "direct" only relative to a cost object — change the object and the same cost can switch between direct and indirect.
Cost of revenue
The non-manufacturing version of COGS — the direct cost of delivering a service (hosting, support, payment fees for software). Same role as COGS, no factory.
Manufacturing overhead
Production costs that can't be pinned to one unit (factory rent, electricity, supervisor pay) but are allocated across units, so they ride inside COGS.
Depreciation
The yearly slice of a long-lived asset's cost, spread over its useful life (€100k machine ÷ 10 yrs = €10k/yr). Lands in COGS for factory assets, in operating expenses for head-office assets.
Variable cost
Moves closely with each sale (raw materials, card fees, shipping). A behaviour, independent of where the cost sits.
Fixed cost
Exists even when sales are low (rent, salaried staff, insurance). Doesn't move unit-by-unit.
Semi-variable (step) cost
Flat across a capacity band, then jumps when capacity must expand (a second support team after 10,000 customers). The realistic shape of most costs.

Efficiency — profit as a share of revenue

Gross margin = gross profit ÷ revenue
How much is kept after direct production costs.
Operating margin = operating profit ÷ revenue
How much is kept after running the business. Usually the most telling single margin.
Net margin = net profit ÷ revenue
Final profit kept per euro of sales.
FCF margin = free cash flow ÷ revenue
Real cash generated per euro of sales. Combines operating quality and cash conversion.

Compounding — what the cash is used for

Reinvestment
Putting cash back into the business (new capacity, R&D, expansion). Good when it earns attractive returns.
Capital allocation
Management's choice among reinvestment, debt repayment, dividends, buybacks, acquisitions, and cash reserves.
ROIC
Return on invested capital — does reinvested money earn an attractive return? The key to "quality compounders."

Valuation — what the market asks you to pay

P/E = price ÷ EPS
Price paid per euro of accounting earnings — i.e. the share price in "years-of-profit." A question generator, not a verdict: read it against earnings quality, growth, cyclicality, and debt.
P/S = market cap ÷ revenue
Price paid per euro of sales.
FCF yield = free cash flow ÷ market cap
Cash return per euro invested in the equity.
Enterprise value (EV) = market cap + debt − cash
Closer to the price of the *whole business*, not just the equity. (Developed fully in the balance-sheet module.)
